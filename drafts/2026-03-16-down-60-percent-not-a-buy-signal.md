A stock down 60% from its all-time high has not become cheap. It has become eligible for investigation.

That distinction is where most value trap entries begin. The investor sees the drawdown, treats it as a valuation signal, and starts building a thesis before asking whether the business is temporarily mispriced or permanently impaired. The drawdown opened the file. It did not answer the question.

EdenFinTech's ATH gate works exactly this way. A stock 60%+ off its peak enters the scanner's universe -- but it still has to clear five first-filter criteria before any thesis is built: solvency, dilution, revenue growth, ROIC, and valuation. Fail any single criterion and the investigation ends. The gate does not open the position. It opens the file.

The natural counter is mean reversion: "Stocks that fall this far are statistically likely to recover." Mean reversion applies to randomly distributed noise, not to structurally impaired businesses. Separating the two is exactly the work the eligibility gate enables but does not complete. The gate narrows the universe to stocks where the market has priced a worst-case narrative. Whether that narrative is accurate or exaggerated requires entirely separate analysis.

Eligibility is not a buy signal. It is the start of work that most investors skip because the drawdown already felt like a conclusion.
